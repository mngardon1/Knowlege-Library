## Three Components. One Integrated Exercise.

Your positioning is built from three things. They're not independent — each one shapes the next. The Upstream Positioning Coach works through all three in a single structured session, in a specific order.

That order matters. POV shapes which problems you can credibly own. The problem you commit to determines who has it most urgently. By the time all three are locked, you have something that is specific, defensible, and distinctly yours.

---

## 1. POV — How You See the World Differently

Your point of view is the foundation. It's the contrarian insight that makes your perspective on your domain distinct from every other consultant who does something similar.

Without a POV, you're describing a service. With one, you're describing a perspective — and perspectives are what buyers remember, repeat, and refer.

> **Go deeper**
>
> A POV isn't a values statement or a mission. It's a specific belief about how your domain actually works — one that many people in your space would push back on or ignore.
>
> It follows a structure: *"Most people believe X. I believe Y. And here's what that means for how I work."*
>
> The POV also names the enemy — the conventional wisdom, broken practice, or default assumption that keeps your buyer stuck. This isn't about being provocative. It's about being clear about what you're working against. Buyers who share your worldview will self-select toward you. Buyers who don't will self-select out. Both outcomes are good.
>
> Your POV comes first because it determines which problems you can credibly own. Two consultants can serve the same market and address the same surface-level problem — but if their worldviews are different, they're actually offering something completely different. The POV is what makes that visible.
>
> Signs your POV is working: a buyer hears it and says "yes, that's exactly what I've been thinking." Signs it isn't: they nod politely and move on.

---

## 2. Problem — What Your Buyer Is Actually Stuck On

Not what you do. Not the outcome you produce. The specific situation your best clients were in when they hired you — described in their words, not yours.

This is the component most consultants get wrong. They describe the problem in solution language: "lack of strategic clarity," "misaligned teams," "inefficient processes." Those aren't problems. Those are consultant diagnoses. Your buyer doesn't experience "lack of strategic clarity." They experience something far more specific and visceral — and that's the language your positioning needs.

> **Go deeper**
>
> A sharp problem statement has three qualities: it's specific enough to exclude people, it's stated in buyer language, and it has a cost — something real that's happening (or not happening) because the problem exists.
>
> The test: could your buyer read the problem statement and think "that's exactly what I've been dealing with"? Not "that's roughly accurate." Exactly.
>
> You'll choose one problem. Not a primary and a secondary. One. The instinct to hedge — "I work with clients on several related issues" — produces invisible positioning. One problem, stated sharply, spoken to the right person, creates immediate recognition. A list creates nothing.
>
> The problem also determines who the person is — which is why it comes second, after POV. Your worldview shapes which problem you can credibly own. The problem you choose determines who has it most urgently.

---

## 3. Person — Who Has That Problem Worst

A profile of your best-fit buyer — not by title or industry, but by situation. What's happening in their world when they become a buyer. What they've already tried. What they're afraid of. What they tell themselves about why the problem exists.

This is where most consultants resist. Defining one specific person means excluding everyone else — and that feels like leaving revenue on the table. It isn't. It's how you become the obvious choice for someone instead of a plausible option for everyone.

> **Go deeper**
>
> The most useful ICP profiles aren't demographic — they're situational. "VP of Sales at a B2B SaaS company with 50-200 employees" is a demographic profile. "A VP of Sales who inherited a team from a founder-led sales motion, is three quarters into missing plan, and is getting pressure from the board to show a new approach" is a situational profile. The second one reads like a person. The first reads like a spreadsheet filter.
>
> You'll also define who you're not for. Not "people who can't afford me" or "companies that are too small" — a specific situation or mindset that makes someone a bad fit regardless of budget. Knowing who you're not for is as important as knowing who you are. It keeps you from taking work that dilutes your positioning and drains your energy.
>
> One ICP. The coach will push you toward a single committed profile. You can expand after you've proven the narrow version works. You cannot un-broaden a vague position.

## What Gets Built

Working through the Upstream Positioning Coach produces six files that go directly into your Upstream OS knowledge library. These are the files every downstream phase draws from.

| File | Folder | What It Contains |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| `narrative-spine.md` | `/Worldview` | POV, contrarian insight, worldview statement, the enemy |
| `core-problem.md` | `/Logic` | One committed problem with buyer evidence and cost of inaction |
| `icp.md` | `/Logic` | Locked ideal client profile — situation, triggers, exclusions |
| `positioning.md` | `/Logic` | Three positioning statement versions + consistency audit |
| `pain-maps.md` | `/Language` | Verbatim buyer language for the problem |
| `desire-maps.md` | `/Language` | Verbatim buyer language for the outcome |

These aren't homework you hand in. They're living assets. Every output your AI OS produces from this point — outreach, content, proposals, sales page copy — draws from these files. The sharper they are, the better everything downstream performs.

## How the Session Works

You'll work through all three components in a single session with the **Upstream Positioning Coach** — a structured AI built specifically for this exercise.

Here's what to expect:

- **It reads your Upstream Diagnostic first.** The coach ingests the file you produced in Week 1 and uses it to identify where to push hardest. Upload it at the start of the session.
- **It asks targeted questions and pushes back.** Safe answers, vague answers, and solutions disguised as problems will be challenged. That's the point. Comfortable positioning is invisible positioning.
- **It works in sequence.** POV → Problem → Person → Synthesis. Each component builds on the last. Don't try to jump ahead.
- **It saves progress at checkpoints.** At the end of each component, the coach tells you exactly which file to save and where. If you need to stop and resume in a new session, upload your saved files and it picks up where you left off.
- **Plan for 60–90 minutes.** You can split it across sessions if needed, but the work flows best when done in one sitting.

Also bring any existing business documents — website copy, a bio, past proposals, a LinkedIn about section. The coach will use them to identify the gap between how you currently describe your work and where your positioning needs to go.
