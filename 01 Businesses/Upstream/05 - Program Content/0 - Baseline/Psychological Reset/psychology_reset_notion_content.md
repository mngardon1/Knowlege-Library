# The Psychology of Positioning

Before you do any positioning work, you need to understand how positioning actually works. Not as a marketing exercise — as a cognitive reality.

This will change how you think about every message you write, every conversation you have, and every decision you make about your business.

---

## The One Thing to Internalize

Positioning doesn't happen on your website. It doesn't happen in your LinkedIn headline. It doesn't happen in your elevator pitch.

**Positioning happens in your buyer's mind.** You don't get to control it. You get to influence it. And if you don't influence it deliberately, the market will assign you a position by default — one you probably won't like.

Every person you talk to is unconsciously asking: *"What mental slot does this person go in?"* If you don't make that easy, they'll either slot you somewhere wrong or forget you entirely.

Your job is to make the right slot obvious.

---

## How Buyer Perception Works

> **The short version:** People organize everything into mental categories and hierarchies. They have limited space. You need to get into the right category and stay there. If you're not in a category, you don't exist to that buyer.

<details>
<summary><strong>Go deeper: The Ladder Model</strong></summary>

Al Ries and Jack Trout — the originators of positioning theory — described buyer perception as a set of mental ladders. For every category a person cares about, they maintain a ladder with a few rungs. Each rung holds a name.

Need a CRM? You think of Salesforce, then HubSpot, maybe a third. Need a project management tool? Asana, Monday, maybe Notion. The ladder has 3-7 rungs, max.

This applies to consultants too. When a VP of Operations thinks "I need help with my team," they have a short mental list — maybe a firm they've used before, a person someone recommended, a name they saw on LinkedIn. That's the ladder.

If you're not on the ladder, you don't exist to that buyer. If you're on the wrong ladder — say, you're slotted as "leadership coach" when you actually do organizational redesign — you're competing against people you shouldn't be compared to.

The strategic choice: get on the right ladder, or build a new one. This program focuses primarily on the first.

</details>

## Why "Good Enough" Positioning Fails

> **The short version:** Vague positioning doesn't get you slotted in the wrong place. It gets you slotted nowhere. The buyer's mind has no room for "consultant who does a lot of things." It has room for "the person who solves X for Y."

<details>
<summary><strong>Go deeper: The Overcommunicated World</strong></summary>

Your buyer is hit with thousands of messages a day. They are not sitting around waiting to carefully evaluate your nuanced value proposition. They will give you about 5 seconds of attention. In those 5 seconds, they need to be able to answer two questions:

1. Is this for me?
2. Is this about my problem?

If the answer to both is "yes" — they lean in. If either answer is unclear, they scroll past. Not because they're not interested. Because their brain literally can't process it fast enough to care.

This is why specificity beats comprehensiveness every time. A brain that hears "I help early-stage consultants who can't close their first 3 clients" processes that instantly. A brain that hears "I'm a strategic advisor helping professionals optimize their business development capabilities" goes blank.

Your positioning needs to work in 5 seconds, or it doesn't work.

</details>

## The Three Traps

Every phase of this program is designed to prevent these three mistakes. Know them now so you can catch yourself making them later.

### Trap 1: Listing Benefits Instead of Solving a Problem

> **The short version:** "I help leaders communicate better" is a benefit floating in space. It doesn't tell anyone why you, why now, or why not someone else. Benefits without context are invisible.

<details>
<summary><strong>Go deeper</strong></summary>

Benefits only have meaning when they're anchored to a specific problem and a specific alternative. "I help you grow revenue" means nothing — everyone claims that. "Unlike a big consulting firm that takes 6 months to deliver a strategy deck, I get you a decision-ready positioning framework in 6 weeks" means something — because it's relative.

Every value claim in your positioning must answer: *compared to what?* If you can't name what it's better than, it's not a value claim. It's a wish.

In the Problem phase, we'll anchor every claim to the buyer's actual experience and the alternatives they've already tried.

</details>

---

### Trap 2: Clever Language Over Clear Language

> **The short version:** If your ideal client can't hear your positioning and immediately understand what you do and who it's for, the language is too clever. Clarity beats creativity at this stage. Every time.

<details>
<summary><strong>Go deeper</strong></summary>

Consultants love to invent terminology. "Transformational Leadership Alignment." "Strategic Capability Acceleration." "Integrated Value Architecture." These phrases feel professional. They feel differentiated. And they are completely meaningless to a buyer who is Googling "why do my best people keep quitting."

The test is simple: say your positioning out loud to someone who fits your ICP. Can they repeat it back in their own words? If they can't, the language is the problem — not their comprehension.

Your buyer's language is always better than your language. In the Person and Problem phases, we'll capture the exact words buyers use to describe their situation. Those words become your positioning language — not the polished consultant version.

</details>

---

### Trap 3: Positioning Too Broad

> **The short version:** "I'm a consultant and I can handle anything you need" feels safe. It's actually invisible. The buyer's mind has no slot for a generalist. You get remembered for specificity or you don't get remembered at all.

<details>
<summary><strong>Go deeper</strong></summary>

Broadening feels like expanding your market. It actually shrinks it. When you say you can help anyone with anything, you trigger no recognition in anyone specific. The VP dealing with a team retention crisis doesn't think "I need a generalist." They think "I need someone who's seen this exact problem before."

Narrowing feels like you're leaving money on the table. In reality, it's the opposite. A narrow position makes you the obvious choice for a specific person — which means faster sales cycles, higher fees, and referrals that land without you having to explain yourself.

The fear is always: "But what if someone outside my niche wants to hire me?" The answer: they still can. Your positioning is your lead message, not your entire capability. You close with a narrow position and expand with the relationship.

In the Person phase, we force this narrowing. One ICP. Not two. Not "primary and secondary." One. You can expand later — but only after you've proven the narrow version works.

</details>

## What to Take Into the Phase Work

You don't need to memorize any of this. But keep three things in your head as you go through the bots:

1. **You're not describing yourself. You're choosing a slot in the buyer's mind.** Every answer should be evaluated against: would my buyer immediately get this?

2. **Specific beats comprehensive.** When in doubt, go narrower. You can always expand later. You can't un-broaden a vague position.

3. **Use their words, not yours.** If it sounds like something you'd put on a consulting slide deck, rewrite it in the language your buyer would use in a text message.

Now move on to your first phase.
