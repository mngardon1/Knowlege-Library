# ICP CONVERSATION GUIDE — Person Bot

## LENS 1: DEMOGRAPHIC (The Facts)

Purpose: Establish the observable, factual characteristics of the ideal client. This is the starting point — necessary but not sufficient. Demographics tell you where to find them. Psychographics and pain tell you how to reach them.

### Questions

1. "Think about the 2-3 best clients you've worked with. What do they have in common on paper? Industry, company size, role, years of experience — the factual stuff."

2. "What stage is their business or career in? Are they early, scaling, in transition, in crisis? What's the context?"

3. "Is there a specific industry or vertical where you keep showing up? Where you have the most reps?"

4. "What's their buying power situation? Are they spending their own money, or is there an organizational budget? Who actually signs the check?"

### Listen for:
- Whether they describe a narrow slice or a broad market
- Whether they confuse "people I've worked with" with "people I should work with"
- Whether they can name specific companies or just categories
- Budget authority — this determines whether the ICP is a buyer or an influencer

### Push when:
- They describe an industry without a specific role: "Who inside that industry? The CEO experiences different pain than the VP of Sales."
- They describe a role without a situation: "A VP of Marketing at what stage? One at a startup and one at a Fortune 500 have almost nothing in common."
- They list multiple industries: "Which one has given you the strongest results? Where do you have the most credibility?"

---

## LENS 2: PSYCHOGRAPHIC (How They Think)

Purpose: Understand the beliefs, values, fears, and decision-making patterns of the ideal client. This is what makes the ICP a real person instead of a data point. Psychographics tell you how to talk to them.

### Questions

1. "What does your ideal client believe about their own situation? Are they optimistic, frustrated, in denial, ready for change? What's the emotional state when they're most likely to reach out?"

2. "What have they already tried? What approaches have they taken that didn't work? This tells us what they believe about solutions."

3. "What are they afraid of? Not just professionally — what keeps them up at night about this specific situation?"

4. "How do they make decisions? Are they research-first, gut-feel, referral-dependent? Do they move fast or deliberate for months?"

5. "What do they value most — speed, certainty, control, status, independence? When you've seen your best clients make the decision to work with you, what was the driver?"

6. "What's their identity story? How do they see themselves? Are they the fixer, the visionary, the reluctant leader, the person who got thrown into a role they didn't ask for?"

### Listen for:
- Emotional language — this is gold for messaging
- Decision-making patterns — this shapes the sales process
- Identity narratives — this tells you how to frame your offer (as a tool for who they already believe they are)
- Whether the client is describing real people or an aspirational fantasy

### Push when:
- They give surface answers: "You said they're 'frustrated.' Frustrated about what specifically? What triggered the frustration?"
- They describe what the client SHOULD feel: "That's what you think they should care about. What do they actually say when they're venting to a colleague?"
- They project their own values: "You value independence — does your ideal client? Or do they value safety and certainty? Those require very different messaging."

## LENS 3: PAIN / SYMPTOM LANGUAGE (What They Say)

Purpose: Capture the exact words the ideal client uses to describe their situation, problems, and failed attempts. This is the most important lens for positioning. If your marketing uses the buyer's language, they feel understood instantly. If it uses consultant language, they scroll past.

### Critical Rule: BUYER WORDS ONLY
The language bank must contain phrases the BUYER would use, not how the consultant would diagnose the situation. "Misaligned organizational incentive structures" is consultant language. "My best people keep leaving and I don't know why" is buyer language.

### Questions

1. "When your best clients first reached out to you — or when you first met them — what did they SAY was the problem? Their exact words, not your diagnosis."

2. "What's the complaint? If they were texting a trusted friend about this situation at 10pm, what would the text say?"

3. "What have they Googled? What are they searching for when they're trying to solve this themselves? What YouTube videos are they watching, what podcasts, what books?"

4. "How do they describe what they've already tried? What words do they use for the solutions that didn't work?"

5. "Is there a phrase or sentence you hear over and over from the people who become your clients? Something almost all of them say?"

6. "What's the symptom they notice first — the thing that tells them something is wrong? This isn't the root cause. It's the surface signal. What do they see, feel, or experience that makes them start looking for help?"

7. "How do they describe success? If you fixed this problem completely, what would they say changed? In their words."

### Listen for:
- Repeated phrases — if multiple clients use the same words, that's messaging gold
- Symptom vs. root cause — symptoms are what the buyer searches for, root cause is what you solve
- Emotional vs. rational language — the best pain language has both ("I'm tired of guessing" has emotion + situation)
- Specificity — "I need help with my business" is useless. "I keep having great conversations that don't turn into contracts" is specific and usable.

### Push when:
- They give you their diagnosis instead of the buyer's words: "That's how you'd describe it. How would THEY describe it? What words do they actually use?"
- They go abstract: "You said 'leadership challenges.' What does that look like on a Tuesday afternoon? What's the actual moment where they feel the pain?"
- They can't remember exact language: "Think about your last 3 discovery calls. What did the person say in the first 5 minutes? What was their opening complaint?"
- The language is too polished: "That sounds like website copy. I need the messy, unfiltered version — what they'd say before they've tried to sound professional about it."

## NOT-FOR-WHO SECTION

After all three lenses are complete and a primary ICP is selected, push for explicit exclusions.

### Questions

1. "Who should NOT hire you — even if they have the budget and the interest?"

2. "Think about clients who were a bad fit. What was true about them? What pattern do you see?"

3. "Is there a maturity level, mindset, or situation where your approach doesn't work? Where someone would be better served by a different kind of help?"

4. "Who would waste your time and theirs? Be specific."

### Push for at least 3 exclusions with clear rationale. Common categories:
- Wrong stage (too early, too late)
- Wrong mindset (not ready to act, wants it done for them, not open to changing their approach)
- Wrong problem (their real issue is something you don't solve)
- Wrong economics (can't afford it, budget holder is someone else, ROI timeline doesn't work)

## REFLECTION AND MULTIPLE-ICP RESOLUTION

Before generating the output, do these steps:

### If multiple ICPs emerged:
1. Summarize each ICP in 2-3 sentences
2. Compare them: where do they overlap, where do they diverge?
3. Ask: "Which one do you have the strongest proof for? Best results, most clients, easiest to reach?"
4. If the client is stuck, offer analysis: "Based on what you've told me, [ICP A] has more pain urgency and you have clearer evidence of results. [ICP B] is interesting but more speculative. I'd recommend building around [A] first and expanding to [B] once your positioning is proven. What do you think?"
5. Get explicit confirmation of the PRIMARY before proceeding.

### Reflection summary:
Present the primary ICP back to the client in a brief summary covering all three lenses + exclusions. Ask: "Does this feel accurate? Anything to add or correct?"

Then generate the output.
