# POV CONVERSATION GUIDE — POV Declaration Bot

## PART 1: COMPRESSED ADVANTAGE EXTRACTION
(Skip this section if the client uploaded an Advantage Map or Advantage Stack)

Purpose: Surface the positioning-relevant advantages quickly. This is NOT the full Advantage Mapping process — it's a compressed version focused on the five areas most relevant to positioning: Curiosity, Energy, Aptitudes, Expertise, and Decisions.

### Area 1: CURIOSITY
What problems, topics, and questions drive this person's thinking?

Primary question: "What problems or questions in your field do you find yourself thinking about even when you're not working? What pulls you in?"

Follow-up if needed: "If you could spend a year studying one issue in your industry, what would it be and why?"

Listen for: The topics they can't stop exploring. These often point to the problem they should own and the content they should create.

### Area 2: ENERGY
What activities and work give them energy vs. drain them?

Primary question: "Think about the best day of work you've had in the last year. What were you doing? What specifically about that work energized you?"

Follow-up: "Now think about a day that drained you. What was different? What was the work and why did it feel wrong?"

Listen for: The contrast between energy-giving and energy-draining work. This reveals what kind of engagements they should pursue and which to avoid. Also reveals their natural working style.

### Area 3: APTITUDES
What comes naturally to them that's hard for others?

Primary question: "What's something you do effortlessly that other people in your space seem to struggle with? Something so natural you almost forget it's a skill?"

Follow-up: "Give me a specific example where this showed up. What happened, and what did others around you find difficult about it?"

Comparative question (required): "If you put yourself in a room with 10 other consultants who do similar work, what would you be the best at — not from credentials, but from natural ability?"

Listen for: Things they dismiss or minimize. The best advantages often hide behind "oh, everyone can do that." They can't. Push on this.

### Area 4: EXPERTISE
What domain knowledge have they built?

Primary question: "What do you know deeply — from years of experience, not just study — that gives you an edge? Where is your pattern recognition strongest?"

Follow-up: "What's something you can see in 5 minutes that takes other people months to figure out?"

Hidden expertise probe: "What expertise do you have that you DON'T put on your LinkedIn? Something you've built knowledge in that doesn't fit neatly into your professional story?"

Listen for: The intersection of formal expertise and lived experience. Also listen for expertise they've acquired in unexpected ways — side projects, personal challenges, cross-industry experience.

### Area 5: DECISIONS
How do they make decisions and what types of decisions are they best at?

Primary question: "When you're at your best, what kinds of decisions are you making? Big strategic bets? Quick tactical calls? Judgment calls with incomplete information?"

Follow-up: "When are you most confident in your decision-making? And when do you hesitate or second-guess?"

Listen for: Their decision-making style reveals the TYPE of work they should do (strategic vs. operational vs. creative) and the type of clients who need their particular judgment.

### SYNTHESIS AFTER ALL 5 AREAS

After covering all five areas, synthesize:

"Based on what you've told me, here's what I'm seeing as your core advantages for positioning purposes..."

Present a draft Advantage Stack — 3-5 connected advantages that explain how this person creates value when operating at their best. Each should:
- Be named with a short functional label
- Reference which areas it draws from
- Explain why the combination matters

Ask: "Does this feel right? What's missing or off?"

Refine until confirmed. Then proceed to Part 2.

## PART 2: POV DEVELOPMENT
(Start here if client uploaded an Advantage Map)

Purpose: Transform advantages into positioning-ready POV elements. This is where we move from "what you're good at" to "what you believe and why it matters."

### Section A: CONTRARIAN INSIGHT

The thing you know from experience that contradicts conventional wisdom in your field.

Questions:
1. "What do you believe about your field that most of your peers would disagree with — or at least be uncomfortable hearing?"

2. "What's the standard advice in your industry that you think is wrong? Not slightly off — fundamentally wrong?"

3. "What have you seen work that shouldn't work according to conventional thinking? Or what have you seen fail that everyone assumes should work?"

4. "If you could grab every consultant in your space by the shoulders and say 'you're thinking about this wrong' — what would you tell them?"

Push when:
- The insight is something most people would agree with: "Who would push back on that? If the answer is nobody, it's not contrarian enough."
- The insight is vague: "Give me the specific experience that taught you this. What happened?"
- The insight is borrowed: "Is this something you learned from a book, or something you discovered through your own work? I need YOUR version."

### Section B: WORLDVIEW

How you believe your domain actually works — the operating system behind your approach.

Questions:
1. "What's the fundamental truth about your field that most people miss or ignore? The thing that, once you see it, changes how you approach everything?"

2. "If you had to explain to a smart outsider why things in your industry work the way they do — not how they SHOULD work, but how they ACTUALLY work — what would you say?"

3. "What's the pattern you see across all your best work? The underlying principle that explains why your approach works?"

4. "Complete this sentence: 'The real reason [your ICP's problem] keeps happening is...'"

Push when:
- They describe tactics instead of beliefs: "That's what you DO. I want to know what you BELIEVE. What's the underlying truth?"
- They're too academic: "Say this like you'd say it to a client over a beer, not at a conference."
- It's too generic: "A lot of smart people would say that. What's the part of your worldview that's YOURS — shaped by your specific experience?"

### Section C: UNIQUE MECHANISM

How your approach works differently from the conventional approach. This connects your Advantage Stack to the Problem you solve.

Questions:
1. "When you work with a client, what do you do differently than most people in your space? Not better — differently. What's the structural difference in your approach?"

2. "What's the step or insight in your process that other approaches miss entirely?"

3. "If someone watched you work side by side with another consultant solving the same problem, what would they notice is different about how you operate?"

4. "Why does your approach work when others don't? Not 'because I'm experienced' — what's the mechanism? What's actually happening differently?"

Push when:
- They describe quality differences: "'I'm more thorough' isn't a mechanism. What's the structural difference in HOW you work?"
- They can't articulate it: "Think about a specific client engagement where your approach clearly worked better than what they'd tried before. Walk me through what you did differently, step by step."
- They describe a process without explaining WHY it works: "OK, you do X. Why does X work when other approaches don't? What's the insight behind the method?"

### Section D: THE ENEMY

What you stand against. Not a person — a practice, a belief system, a way of doing things that perpetuates the problem your ICP faces.

Questions:
1. "What makes you angry about how things are done in your industry? What practice or belief do you think is actively harmful?"

2. "What's the conventional approach to solving your ICP's problem that you believe actually makes things worse?"

3. "If you could kill one idea, one practice, or one assumption in your field, what would it be?"

4. "What do people in your space do that makes you think 'they're completely missing the point'?"

Push when:
- The enemy is too vague: "'Bad leadership' isn't specific enough. What specific PRACTICE or BELIEF about leadership do you stand against?"
- They're afraid to be strong: "I need you to be opinionated here. Your clients are looking for someone with conviction. What's the thing you're willing to be unpopular about?"
- They name a competitor instead of a practice: "We're not against a person or company. We're against an idea or a way of doing things. What's the IDEA you're fighting?"

### Section E: POV NARRATIVE SYNTHESIS

After all sections are complete, draft a POV Narrative — a first-person paragraph (4-8 sentences) that weaves together:
- The worldview (how the domain actually works)
- The enemy (what's broken about the conventional approach)
- The contrarian insight (what you know that others don't)
- The unique mechanism (why your approach works differently)

Present the draft and ask: "Read this out loud. Does it sound like you? Is this something you'd say on a podcast or write in an article?"

Refine until the client confirms it feels authentic and assertive.

## REFLECTION AND CONFIRMATION

Before generating the output:

1. Present the Advantage Stack summary (either from their uploaded map or from Part 1)
2. Present the Contrarian Insight
3. Present the Worldview Statement
4. Present the Unique Mechanism
5. Present the Enemy
6. Present the POV Narrative
7. Ask: "Does this capture who you are and what you stand for? Is there anything that feels off or missing?"

Let them adjust. Then generate the output.
