# Hook Design Guide

## Purpose
Hooks exist to **create recognition before resistance activates**.

They are not meant to:
- teach
- convince
- explain
- prove intelligence

A hook has **one job only**:

> Accurately name lived pain so the reader feels seen before their defenses come online.

Hooks operate exclusively at the **Pain** layer of the Content Crosswalk.  
They do not resolve the pain, explain the solution, or introduce frameworks.

---

## Core Hook Principles

Effective hooks for this system must:
- feel observed, not marketed
- feel true, not impressive
- feel safe, not demanding
- reduce pressure rather than create urgency

Clarity beats cleverness.  
Recognition beats persuasion.

---

## Psychological Triggers (Primary Levers)

### 1. Identity Tension (Not Outcome Hype)

The audience is not primarily motivated by money or status.  
They are motivated by the quiet tension of unrealized potential and misalignment.

Effective hooks surface an **identity split**:
- who they are rewarded for being  
- vs who they sense they could become

Use:
- “You’re not lazy. You’re misaligned.”
- “You don’t hate work. You hate wasted potential.”
- “Your job isn’t the problem. Staying unconscious in it is.”

Avoid:
- income promises
- escape fantasies
- speed-based outcomes

---

### 2. Named Pain > Promised Transformation

This audience trusts **diagnosis more than prescription**.

If they say “that’s exactly it,” they will keep reading.

Effective hooks name invisible or unspoken pain:
- identity drift
- borrowed ambition
- quiet resentment
- cognitive dissonance
- mental load

Examples:
- “Most corporate burnout isn’t from too much work. It’s from too little meaning.”
- “You’re not stuck because you lack options. You’re stuck because every option costs identity.”

---

### 3. Permission, Not Pressure

The audience does not need motivation.  
They need **permission** to think differently without blowing up their life.

Effective hooks offer relief first.

Examples:
- “You don’t need to quit your job to start becoming someone else.”
- “Nothing is wrong with you for wanting more and stability.”
- “You’re allowed to outgrow a role that once saved you.”

---

## Approved Hook Structures (With Stage Constraints)

### 1. Bold Identity Reframe

**Best for:** Break-stage content

**Purpose:**  
Collapse a complex emotional truth into a single sentence that feels inevitable.

**Structure:**
- Absolute statement
- Identity implication
- No explanation

**Rules:**
- One idea only
- No framework names
- No outcomes or CTAs

Examples:
- “Most people don’t hate their job. They hate who they have to be to succeed in it.”
- “Stability isn’t safety if it costs you self-trust.”

---

### 2. Problem → Pattern Hook (Subtle PAS)

**Best for:** Break-stage or early Become-stage content

**Purpose:**  
Reveal the hidden pattern behind a familiar pain.

**Structure:**
- Name the problem
- Reveal the pattern
- Imply another way exists (do not explain)

**Rules:**
- Pattern must be psychological or identity-based
- No tactical language
- No solutions introduced

Example:
- “A pattern I see in high performers who feel stuck: they followed every rule and never chose themselves.”

---

### 3. Before / After Identity Contrast

**Best for:** Become-stage content

**Purpose:**  
Signal lived experience and credibility without superiority.

**Structure:**
- Before state (internal)
- After state (clarity, not lifestyle)
- Bridge teased, not taught

**Rules:**
- No flexing
- No outcome signaling
- Focus on identity, not results

Example:
- “I used to think something was wrong with me for wanting more. Now I see it was a signal, not a flaw.”

---

### 4. Conflict With the Cultural Script

**Best for:** Break-stage or Build-stage (used carefully)

**Purpose:**  
Create differentiation by naming a broken assumption.

**Rules:**
- Conflict with ideas, not people
- No antagonism
- No moral superiority

Examples:
- “Most career advice is designed to keep systems stable, not people fulfilled.”
- “The advice to ‘just be grateful’ keeps more people stuck than any job ever could.”

---

## Language Rules for Hooks (Non-Negotiable)

Use:
- absolutes (“most people,” “almost no one,” “the real reason”)
- short sentences
- contrast
- identity language (who you are becoming vs who you’ve been)

Avoid:
- buzzwords without context
- teaching in the hook
- tactics or steps
- multiple ideas in one hook
- conjunction stacking (“and” usually signals dilution)

---

## Framework Usage Rule (Critical)

Framework names **never appear in hooks**.

Frameworks stabilize meaning **after attention is earned**.  
Hooks remain human, immediate, and unabstracted.

---

## Internal Quality Check

Before keeping a hook, ask:

1. Would my audience feel seen or sold to?
2. Does this create relief or pressure?
3. Is the enemy internal or systemic (not personal failure)?
4. Would this still work if there were no CTA after it?

If all four are true → keep it.  
If not → rewrite.

---

## Final Principle

Most people try to write hooks that impress.

This system requires hooks that tell the truth faster than the reader can rationalize it away.

That is the edge.
