# The Reclamation
## Individual Participant Responsibility in the Future of Human Capitalism
*Michael Gardon | Feb 24, 2026*

---

Parts I and II of this series were a diagnosis. This part isn't.

It's about YOUR role in moving forward. Because the uncomfortable truth buried inside everything we've covered is: waiting for the system to fix itself is the same passive move that got us here.

If you feel The Crisis laid out in Part II, the path back doesn't run through HR or Washington. It runs through you. And it always did.

Despite capitalism's "brokenness" - hierarchy, bureaucracy, power asymmetry, layoffs, AI - opportunity is everywhere, if you can see it. And seeing it can only happen if we reclaim our dynamism. We need a Self-Directed Renaissance. A fundamental reclamation of what was severed — your agency in deciding your future.

## Your Locus of Control

The real result of The Severance is a blurring between what you control and what you don't. We've allowed organizations to shape our goals, define our value, manage our career paths, and absorb our risk. This process is productive, and leads to opportunity, but what happens when reality does not match where we thought we were going? Our sense of agency suffers. Inside the system that has cultivated us, it becomes harder and harder to see alternative pathways. Turns out, when you're credentialed, pathed and resumed, you view your options only from that limited lens. Your internal sense of control over what you do next or what you could do became much smaller.

External shocks happen to us. Restructuring happens to you. AI happens to you. The market shifts and it happens to you. The layoff. The stalled career. The missed opportunity. Sometimes even the internal brooding feeling of "something's off" can feel external. These are facts of life. The question isn't: "how should the system stop these from happening?" — That's out of your control. The real question you should ask is: "how do I make sure my goals are my own, pursue them, AND not allow anything to take me off course?"

My answer is Self-Direction.

It starts with a simple, uncomfortable claim: I am responsible for where I am, and I am responsible for where I go. Not the company. Not the economy. Not the timing. Me.

That's not a denial of real structural forces. But causation and responsibility are different things. You didn't cause the system to drift. You are still responsible for your response to it.

At first, this can feel heavy and pressure packed, maybe even limiting. But as we unpack Self-Direction, it becomes extremely liberating.

All over the world we see examples of self-direction. Normal people taking control, going their own way and building the lives they want. They're breaking off from the static structures that gave them a start, but no longer serve their dynamic vision of their lives.

This started happening long before Covid and the dawn of AI.

It's been happening and will continue to happen as a response to the problems outlined in this series.

This movement I call the Self-Directed Renaissance. But you can't get from where you are to what you see on Instagram, YouTube or any other glimpse you get without a fundamental reorientation in your responsibility and what you control.

So what can you do? Turns out, you have much more control than you think. It starts by reclaiming control over four levers:

**Your time. Your value. Your risk. Your options.**

## The Time Lever

Of all the things The Severance impacted, our relationship to time is the most insidious loss — because we agreed to it so cleanly.

Time is the only true asset you have. You were born with it. It predates your degree, your title, your employer. It requires no capital and no permission. Every decision we make has an impact on our time.

Time only moves in one direction. Its finite nature means it is the most valuable asset you have.

When an asset has high value, we seek to own it.

Oil. Gold. Diamonds. Real estate.

But we don't do this with time. Instead we sell it, or worse, give it away.

We sell our time to the highest bidder. We gain marketable skills, get a salary, derive a per hour "worth", and seek ways to increase that per hour "worth."

This transaction is completely rational and useful. What is less rational and useful is where this transaction leaves many of us. Busier than ever, with fewer options. More responsibility and less time. Overcommitted and burned out.

The majority of people respond by trying to manufacture more time (productivity culture). "How do I do more?" But you can't manufacture more time. Even if you could, the transaction would find ways to eat it up.

The answer isn't mechanical, it's attitudinal.

**Start treating your time like you own it.**

The ownership mentality recognizes that time is finite. Not: "how do I do more?", but "how do I allocate my time to its highest use?" Answering that question liberates rather than constrains.

### Dynamic Time Value

The employment contract has given us a static value of time. You're paid a salary, and from that you can calculate a fixed per hour value of your time. With this fixed value, each obligation that comes across your plate can be seen as having the same value.

A meeting with 14 other people can be easily viewed the same as an hour of focused work on your objective. When time is viewed this way, scope creep happens. We start saying yes to distraction, stop and chats, and extra initiatives because "hey, I spend 9 hours a day here, whatever they want to give me, I'll say yes to."

But time doesn't have a fixed value. It has a directed value. The value of our time is dynamic, meaning it can have different values based on how we direct that time.

Viewed through this lens, sitting in a meeting and not saying anything has a very different value than using that same hour to move your objective forward. Which has a very different value than if you used that same hour to deeply work on a side project or plot a strategic career move.

When you begin to see this difference, you can start to make different choices — to create space for higher quality work, more balance and peace, or to further a personal goal more deeply tied to meaning.

*"But my company owns my time!"* No they don't. What they've bought is the outcomes you deliver. Hours are just an accounting mechanism.

People who learn to protect their time and focus on high directed value create space, lower distraction and reclaim up to 40% of their time from their jobs.

Everything else in this reclamation — your value, your risk awareness, your options — flows from how you relate to your time. It's the foundation because it's the only thing that was always, entirely, irreversibly yours.

The question was never how much your time is worth.

**It's who decides what it's for.**

## The Value Lever

Your value is directly derived from your time and how you've directed that time. If most of your time and direction have been inside the container of a company, then by definition, your view of your value is constrained by the container — and reinforced by the visual artifacts of that journey.

How do you think about your own value? I'm quite sure you have a feeling that your value is more than what your resume says. The trouble has been porting that value into a different context.

Your title. Your resume. Your LinkedIn profile. Your performance review. Your years of experience in a narrow field. These artifacts aren't lies. They represent real work, real contribution, real capability. But they are a translation of who you are and what you can produce. Like any translation, something is always lost.

What's lost in translation is the clear view of your underlying capability. What you actually do is only a fragment of what you are capable of. The rest has been ground down or sheared off by the container as it gets narrower and narrower.

You've allowed the employment container to become the sole interpreter of your value. And when your value only exists inside one container, you become fragile. The container changes — restructuring, automation, strategic pivots — and suddenly you don't know what you're worth or where you'd go.

The answer is to own your full value and how you communicate it to the world.

What do you actually care about? What are you motivated by? Who are you when you're doing your best work? When are you most dynamic and alive? What is the underlying capability your employer is paying for — and what would that be worth somewhere else, or on your own terms?

Answering these questions clearly is an act of ownership. It makes your value portable. And portable value is the difference between someone who gets displaced by the system and someone who moves through it deliberately.

Once you see it, then your responsibility becomes communicating this value in a way that attracts the right opportunities.

Done with passion and gusto, you become a human again, not just another line item on a spreadsheet.

Owning your value doesn't just change how you present yourself — it changes what becomes possible. The opportunities you couldn't predict. The doors that open sideways. That's the dynamic promise restored at the individual level.

## The Risk Lever

You have to take back a sense of your exposure, and what you can do about it. The first step is confronting reality.

Employment paths no doubt reduce risk, but how much exposure employees are left with varies widely. Risk isn't binary. The employment path isn't safe just as much as starting a business isn't risky. Both are overblown.

Most employees in the system acknowledge, at some level, that bad and unforeseen things can happen.

Layoffs. AI displacement. Industry consolidation. A new CEO with a different vision. A merger that eliminates your entire function. These aren't hypotheticals — they're regular occurrences in the modern economy. Most people nod at this reality and move on — hoping it will never happen to them.

What they can actually do about it is another story.

### Problem 1: Risk Blindness

If employees give a conscious thought to risk management, it's usually in the form of loyalty. Work harder. Go the extra mile. Build a reputation inside the organization. Stay visible. Be indispensable.

Proving yourself indispensable so you won't get fired (first) feels like risk management. It isn't.

Loyalty and effort are real and valuable — but they have no bearing on exogenous shocks. When a structural shock hits — a market contraction, an AI-driven restructuring, a strategic pivot — the organization reduces its human contribution to a line item on a spreadsheet. Loyalty doesn't appear on that spreadsheet. Years of service don't appear on that spreadsheet. The extra miles don't either.

Worse, the loyalty strategy actually concentrates your risk rather than diversifying it. Let's be honest — you're going the extra mile because of an incentive structure that promises you future opportunity (and dollars) for it. This is how they get you to stay. Every year stayed gets harder to leave.

You were promised future reward for present loyalty. But the system that made that promise can't guarantee its delivery.

The wrong risk is managed.

### Problem 2: Loss Aversion

While risk blindness can lead us to manage the wrong risk, it also constrains us from seeing payoffs clearly.

We hear "risk" and we think "bad."

But risk is actually variance — it has a downside and an upside.

In 1979, psychologist Daniel Kahneman demonstrated that humans feel losses roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains. We will work harder to avoid losing $100 than to gain $100 — even when the math is identical.

He called it loss aversion bias. It's logically irrational, and it quietly skews how we make almost every decision.

It's why you stay in the meeting you should skip. Why you don't raise your hand for the stretch assignment. Why you don't leave the job that's slowly suffocating you. Why you set small, achievable goals instead of ambitious ones.

We see and feel the pain viscerally, but the payoff is somehow abstract.

The outcome of this bias over a 30 year employment career is that we take less and less risk by setting smaller and smaller goals.

We conform, we settle, we don't push back. We filter our options by what's realistic instead of what's possible. "I can't possibly change careers because I have so much already invested." Or, "I can't possibly start my own business, because business is risky."

We systematically overvalue what we have to lose and undervalue what we have to gain. The result is that our options set for what we can do and where we could take our value is massively constrained.

**Loss aversion makes you blind to the upside.**

### Towards The Agentic Risk Relationship

Your personal risk isn't just exposure to a bad outcome. It's the full range of what could happen — in both directions. Even an objectively bad outcome — losing a job — can have an unexpected, but high payoff. Most people I work with who have been laid off report huge personal and professional growth in the months after. This forcing function pushed them into a perspective shift that got them taking ownership and agency over their circumstances. These folks have gone on to build the businesses they'd been thinking about for years or have gone on to completely reinvent their careers. Almost all of them wished they made this change much earlier.

The moral of the story is that "risk" has an upside — and a big one, but only if you allow it to.

Here are 3 specific ways you can take more agency over your relationship with risk:

**1. Take your exposure to job loss as fact.** You don't have to move to the wilderness and become a prepper. Just allow for the fact that it can happen to anyone, especially in times of fast disruption. This alone can help you start to think more dynamically about your future.

**2. Inventory optimizations in your life that leave you fragile or exposed.** Too much optimization can constrain your options and resilience toward stressors. Examples:

- Optimized long term investing strategy that leaves you illiquid
- Over-optimized schedule that leaves you productive at the expense of strategic thinking and planning time
- Over-reliance on your employer — have you drank the Kool-Aid? What happens if they don't hold up their end of the bargain?
- Optimized domain expertise — do you feel pigeonholed? Listen to that feeling.
- Optimized identity — are you and your work synonymous? What happens to the "you" if work changes?

**3. Start creating options.** Options reduce risk. Not "plan B," "plan C," or "plan D." You don't need perfect plans, you need optionality — which is the topic of the next section.

What risk-aware people do differently is that they don't avoid variance. They position themselves so that over time, more breaks go their way than against them.

Which brings us to the final lever.

## The Optionality Lever

If you control your time better, understand your value and understand your risk, you've reclaimed much of your psychological agency, but we still need to translate that to action to find better opportunity for growth within the system.

Optionality is the leading edge of that action.

Not a 20 point plan B for if or when you get fired. Not a 5 year, meticulous plan to change your life.

Optionality is the practice of keeping real pathways in motion — so that as the world changes, you're already moving with it rather than being moved by it.

It's how you use your reclaimed agency to punch through your current circumstances. How you expand your luck surface area so more good things find their way to you. How you break out of the static cage — not by blowing it up, but by building doors in the walls while you're still inside.

### What Optionality Actually Does

Optionality works in two ways:

1. As a risk reduction strategy (diversification)
2. As a leveraged upside bet (toward growth)

What most people don't realize is that optionality works both ways at the same time.

**As Risk Reduction**

If you see your employment risk clearly, you know the restructuring doesn't ask who stayed late. The AI displacement doesn't check your performance reviews. The merger doesn't care about your tenure.

These events tend to leave people scrambling for the job posting sites and spinning them up into frantic, existential crises. But not you — because you're practicing optionality.

They get a calendar invite. "Quick chat with HR." They know dread immediately. Their identity and everything they've worked for is called into question. After, they're sitting in their car in the parking lot with a box of desk items and a phone full of contacts they've never actually cultivated. They've lived paycheck to paycheck assuming it would continue, now it isn't.

You get the same calendar invite. Same parking lot. But you've been investing time and energy having coffee with people outside your company and industry for the past year. You've got a bit of cash stashed away for just this purpose. You've been writing, building, creating something the world can see. Your value is legible beyond their org chart. Three people already know you well enough to make a call on your behalf. One conversation is already halfway to something real.

The shock is still there. But the scramble isn't.

That's the difference optionality makes — not that bad things don't happen, but that when they do, you're already in motion rather than starting from zero.

**As a Leveraged Upside Bet**

The more powerful way optionality works is offensive. When your view of the world changes — you want new horizons, you've done well on a pathway but want something "more," but can't quite define "more."

Optionality practice helps you experiment your way there, instead of picking a path off a menu.

*Example 1:* You've thought about entrepreneurship, or a role outside of your current domain. Both feel unknown and risky. But you've been having coffee with people outside your company and industry for the past year. One of those conversations turns into something unexpected — a problem they need solved, an external role that didn't exist until you walked in the room, a partnership that makes both of you better. You didn't plan it. You couldn't have. But you were in motion, so when it appeared you were already facing the right direction.

*Example 2:* You've been writing, building, developing a unique point of view and creating something the world can see. Someone finds it. They reach out. They already understand your value before you've said a word. The conversation starts at a completely different level than any job application ever could.

*Example 3:* You've been having one honest conversation each week with yourself about what's pulling you forward and what isn't. Slowly, a picture emerges — not of a perfect plan, but of a direction. Something that fits better than what you have now. And because you've been building toward it in small ways, it doesn't feel like a leap anymore. It feels like the next step.

None of these outcomes were predictable. All of them were made possible by optionality already in motion.

This is the mechanism by which optionality punches through loss aversion bias. Each conversation returns signal. Each piece of external work is evidence that your value travels. Each small bet expands what feels possible — about the market, about what you want, about what you're actually capable of when you test yourself against something real.

These things compound. Slowly at first, then noticeably.

You're not just reducing downside anymore. You're expanding your luck surface area — making yourself visible, making yourself discoverable, making yourself available to opportunities you couldn't have predicted or planned for.

That's optionality at full capacity. Not insurance. Not escape.

Compounding bets on your own dynamism and the possibility of where it could lead.

### The Failure Mode

Optionality has a shadow side worth naming. Too many options and there's no action.

This is the dreamer trap.

The dreamer sees potentiality, but stays there — never converting potentiality to a real option. The dreamer accumulates possibilities, commits to nothing, and mistakes motion for progress.

A podcast. A consulting practice. A course. A newsletter. A new industry to explore. Three coffees to schedule.

It's so easy to fall into this trap in modern times.

But real optionality isn't a thing, it's a practice. Generating inspiration, synthesizing what it means, and then selecting and attending to that signal. Resulting in a small number of real pathways, pursued with genuine curiosity, updated by what you actually learn. The signal that comes back — from conversations, from what you create, from what pulls you forward and what doesn't — is what shapes your direction over time.

The dreamer ignores signal in favor of novelty. The option-builder listens to it.

## The Reclamation Outcome: Individual Dynamism Restored

What I've proposed here is a systematic pathway for individuals to restore agency and control, thereby increasing their participation in making the future of human capitalism better.

This viewpoint does not touch on what the system itself can or should do — only the individual.

The levers proposed are time, value, risk and optionality. When used together, they act as a system — each lever activating the next, each one expanding your locus of control further than the last.

It starts with time ownership. When you reclaim sovereignty over how your time is directed, something shifts immediately. You stop being a passive recipient of other people's agendas and start making conscious choices about what your hours produce. That shift is psychological before it's practical. It changes how you see everything that follows.

With directed time comes the space to know your value. Not the container's translation of it — your actual underlying capability, made visible and portable. When you know what you can genuinely do and what it's worth in the world, you stop needing the organization to tell you who you are. Your identity detaches from your title. Your confidence stops being borrowed from your employer, and you start to seek more aligned ways to express that value.

With portable value comes the ability to see risk clearly. Not the distorted version — not the loss-aversion-inflated danger of change, not the risk-blind false safety of staying. The honest version. What you're actually exposed to. What the system can and cannot guarantee. What staying costs you that you've been too comfortable to count.

And with clear risk view comes the capacity to build real options. Not fantasies. Not escape hatches. Pathways already in motion — fueled by your time, built on your value, calibrated by your honest reading of risk. Options that reduce your fragility and expand your possibility simultaneously.

Each lever compounds the others. More time sovereignty means deeper value clarity. Deeper value clarity means more accurate risk assessment. More accurate risk assessment means better options. Better options mean more agency to affect outcomes.

This is an expanding loop — the opposite of the contracting loop the severance created.

And here's what that loop produces when you run any decision through it honestly: the options that felt impossible start to look different. Change looks less dangerous than loss aversion suggested. Staying looks less safe than risk blindness implied. The ceiling you accepted turns out to be lower than your actual capability. The leap you've been postponing turns out to be a step you're already halfway through.

This is your decision lens going forward. Not a checklist — a way of seeing. Every opportunity, every risk, every fork in the road gets run through these four questions:

1. What does this require of my time?
2. Does this connect to my real value?
3. What is the honest risk here — both sides of the variance?
4. Does this open more doors or close them?

Answer those honestly and the path forward gets clearer than it's been in years.
