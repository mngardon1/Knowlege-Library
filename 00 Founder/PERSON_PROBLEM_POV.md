## The Core Argument

Most consultants fail to build a pipeline not because they lack skill — but because they lack specificity. They cast a wide net to avoid leaving opportunity on the table. This is rational and wrong.

The wide net produces vague positioning. Vague positioning produces vague conversations. Vague conversations don't close — because the buyer can't quickly decide if you're the right specific person for their problem, and can't sell you internally to other decision makers.

This is the job interview trap: morphing and twisting your value to fit what you think the buyer wants, the same way you'd shape yourself to a job description. It muddies the water. You become interchangeable.

The fix is upstream of marketing and delivery. You don't have a tactics problem. You have a Product-Market Fit problem. And you can't solve PMF with better outreach — you solve it with positioning first.

**PPP is the positioning system: Person → Problem → POV.**

---

## The Three Components

### 1. PERSON
Not a demographic. A specific human being in a specific context.

- Weak: "CTOs at mid-size companies"
- Strong: "A CTO at a $100M company managing a 30-person engineering team"

The more specific, the scarier it feels. The scarier it feels, the more it's working. Specificity is the mechanism of resonance — the right person reads it and thinks *this is for me*, not *this might apply to me.*

**How to find the Person:** Who have you done your best work for? Who energized you? Who got the best results? Start there — not with who you *could* serve.

---

### 2. PROBLEM
Not a category. A specific, named, felt problem.

- Weak: "Overall corporate AI strategy"
- Strong: "Can't get his leadership team aligned on using AI for [specific initiative]"

The problem must be something the Person already knows they have. Not something you diagnose after the fact. If they don't feel it before they meet you, you'll spend the whole conversation convincing them they have a problem instead of solving it.

**The problem test:** Can the buyer describe this problem in their own words before you said anything? If yes — you have a real problem. If you have to explain why they have it — keep digging.

---

### 3. POV
The intersection of your unique advantages, a contrarian insight, a worldview about how your domain actually works, and a mechanism that explains why your approach works when conventional approaches don't.

This is also where you name your Enemy — not a person, but a practice or belief that keeps your ICP stuck. The Enemy gives you something to stand against. That's what turns a positioning statement into a platform.

**The four POV elements:**

**Contrarian Insight** — What you know from experience that contradicts conventional wisdom. Must be specific enough that peers would push back. If nobody would disagree, it's not contrarian — it's common sense.

**Worldview** — The fundamental truth about your domain that most people miss. Not tactics. The operating premise that everything else follows from. Should feel like the opening of a manifesto.

**Unique Mechanism** — How your approach works *differently*, not just better. Structural difference, not qualitative. "I'm more thorough" is not a mechanism. "I start with advantage extraction before positioning, which surfaces differentiation that market-first approaches miss" is a mechanism.

**The Enemy** — The practice, belief, or way of doing things you stand against. Must be specific. "Bad leadership" is too vague. "The obsession with 'best practices' that trains consultants to sound identical and compete on price" is an enemy.

**POV output:** A first-person narrative (4-8 sentences) the consultant could read aloud on a podcast or at the opening of a keynote. Not LinkedIn copy. Not a bio. Conviction.

---

## Why POV Is the Hard Part

Person and Problem are findable through reflection and specificity. POV requires conviction — and most consultants have strong beliefs they've never been forced to articulate. They've been in corporate environments where strong opinions were softened. They default to safe.

The POV bot's job (and Michael's job in coaching) is to surface the beliefs that are already there and make them concrete. When a consultant says something genuinely sharp: *"That's it. That's a real POV. Most people in your space wouldn't say that."* When they give mush: *"That's too safe. I need the version that would make someone in your industry uncomfortable."*

---

## What PPP Produces Downstream

With clear PPP:
- Every conversation starts from a position of specificity, not negotiation
- The buyer can quickly self-qualify: "yes, that's me and that's my problem"
- Internal selling becomes easy: the buyer can explain you to the other decision makers
- Content has a clear target — you're writing to one person with one problem
- The pipeline builds on signal, not volume

Without PPP:
- More tactics, less traction
- Every marketing experiment fails at the same root level
- Harder work, not better results

**The leverage point is upstream. Fix positioning first. Everything downstream gets easier.**

## The Bot Architecture (POV Phase)

The POV bot runs in two parts:

**Part 1 — Compressed Advantage Extraction** (if no Advantage Map exists): Covers Curiosity, Energy, Aptitudes, Expertise, and Decisions. Surfaces the 3-5 advantages that matter for positioning.

**Part 2 — POV Development**: Builds Contrarian Insight, Worldview, Unique Mechanism, and Enemy. Synthesizes into POV Narrative.

**Output:** A POV Declaration document — the seed for all content, thought leadership, and sales conversations. Every article, post, and outreach message should flow from this worldview.

**Quality rules for POV output:**
- Contrarian Insight must be experience-grounded, not borrowed
- Worldview is a premise, not a tactic
- Unique Mechanism is structural, not qualitative
- Enemy is specific and opinionated
- POV Narrative sounds like a human with conviction, not a LinkedIn bio
- Content Seeds are arguable angles, not generic topics
