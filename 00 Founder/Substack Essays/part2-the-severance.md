# The Severance
## Part 2 in a 5 Part Series on The Future Of Human Capitalism
*Michael Gardon | Feb 18, 2026*

---

In Part 1, I showed how capitalism's employment promise shifted from dynamic to static. But I didn't fully articulate what that shift severed or what the ramifications are for us today.

It severed agency from the human experience.

Our system is so highly developed and optimized that it presents choices that masquerade as agency.

- choose a major
- choose a field
- choose a job role
- choose from a (limited) advancement set

Your early "choice" starts to groove the course of the rest of your life. Most choices aren't yours anyway. They're heavily shaped and influenced by other factors.

Seriously, ask yourself honestly: did you select your own goals? Or have they become selected and shaped for you? Is your striving going toward something you value and feel a sense of accomplishment for? If not, can you change?

The truth is, you can change, but late change becomes extremely hard. Because, like a river cutting through sandstone, the more time you give it, the deeper it gets. One day you wake up at the bottom of the Grand Canyon, staring up at a thousand-foot cliff.

What's unacknowledged in society is that humans are, by definition, dynamic. We change over time. Who we are, what we want, what we value, what we care about—all that changes.

What you wanted at 18 is not the same as what you want now. Nor should it be!

But the system we operate in is static. What is valued on a resume or in an interview is progression. Options appear limited in this context, and our agency suffers.

Turns out, agency is the bleeding edge of what powers human dynamism. When it's lost, stagnation is the result.

---

## The Agency Feedback Loop

Agency creates a feedback loop. When people can make meaningful choices, they experiment. Experimentation produces learning. Learning builds competence. Competence reinforces ownership. Ownership strengthens motivation. When agency is removed, this loop breaks. Compliance replaces experimentation. Risk avoidance replaces learning. Plateau replaces growth. Disengagement becomes rational.

This is the essence of "being stuck." That feeling of stagnation that so many can't explain. It's not just a personal problem; it's a societal one.

We are driven by the future and our prospects of it (Prospect Theory).

We are motivated by selecting our own goals and striving toward them.

In today's system, millions of us become pre-sorted. We're filtered by education, upbringing, comparison, and roles. At some point, we realize: "our goals" have become selected for us.

The static—*what's good for the company is good for you*—replaced the dynamic—*who do I want to be?*

The dynamic goal has the most agency embedded in it. It has the most optionality embedded in it, and it has the most possibility embedded in it.

Humans are natural growth and adaptation engines.

We seek development.

When we believe our abilities can grow in ways we select, we show:

- Better performance
- Higher motivation
- Higher resilience
- Greater long-term achievement
- Higher life satisfaction

But only if reinforced by environment.

Research shows humans continue seeking development across their lifespans.

In midlife and beyond, even highly accomplished people experience distress if they are not:

- Building
- Contributing
- Evolving

In a word, they experience stagnation. They feel static. According to the research, stagnation correlates with:

- Meaninglessness
- Depression
- Cynicism

My view is that stagnation—the loss of agency—is the root problem in our society today.

---

## The Tension of the Times We Live In

The blurriness of agency would be a problem by itself, but there's an added, very interesting wrinkle that makes this time period unique for a full-blown crisis.

The modern worker is now exposed to more information about their human possibilities than ever existed before.

We read about the "overnight success."

We study the unicorn creators.

We scroll past example after example on social media.

We can get 10 steps to build a business at the click of a button.

It has literally never been easier in the history of the world to see examples of people using agency to set their own goals and create.

This should be a good thing, but it's actually a psychological problem.

We are able to see "success" stories from dynamic capitalism on Instagram or in the news. Creators and builders taking non-traditional paths and getting wealthy while owning their time. Yet, our pathways seem limited by the static view we've succeeded in.

Social comparison theory shows that:

- Exposure to alternative identities increases dissatisfaction with one's current state.
- Awareness of possible selves increases tension if not pursued.

We now live in high "possible selves" visibility environments, at exactly the same time our identities have cemented on our narrow, static pathways.

That is new in human history.

---

## The Crisis

What has happened is predictable.

The psychological gap that is always present has been cracked wide open.

It's plain for an outside observer to see:

The gap between who a person wanted to become and who their lives allow them to be.

The innate, inner drive for dynamism, growth, and self-direction (which they thought they were following through prescriptive choices, lanes, and stability) collides directly with a realization that their path wasn't following their dream at all.

What happens when you've worked for 20 years, made money, built a comfortable life, acquired praise and credentials, but feel trapped, unfulfilled, stagnant, and frustrated?

It's a conflict that persists unnamed and unresolved sometimes for decades because it's a foreign problem. School doesn't have a course for this. And no one has a step-by-step way out with certainty that you'll come out better on the other side.

It festers, gets worse, and develops into a crisis of meaning.

> *"This is life."*
> *"Changing is too risky."*
> *"I don't have any options."*
> *"Nothing I do matters anyway."*

Without agency or development, depression, anxiety, apathy, and low engagement persist.

## The Metastasis

When this conflict appears in one life, it looks personal. When it appears in millions, it becomes structural. What begins as private dissatisfaction scales into collective underperformance.

These consequences reinforce each other. Misalignment produces disengagement. Disengagement invites managerial control. Control creates complexity. Complexity deepens misalignment.

What is still described as an individual problem has metastasized into a societal problem with wide-ranging ramifications.

### 1. $8T in Lost Productivity Per Year

Low engagement and worker disengagement are economic costs at scale.

According to Gallup's State of the Global Workplace report, disengaged and actively disengaged employees are estimated to cost the global economy about $8.8 trillion annually — roughly 9% of global GDP.

OECD and McKinsey research similarly finds that skills mismatch, disengagement, and low discretionary effort are major contributors to productivity stagnation in advanced economies.

In the United States specifically, Gallup estimated that disengagement accounted for around $1.9 trillion in lost productivity in 2023 alone.

Even short-term drops in engagement have measurable effects: in 2024, a mere two-point decline in engagement was associated with about $438 billion in lost productivity worldwide.

These figures aren't abstract. They reflect hours of labor, creative contribution, and economic output that never materialize because large swaths of employees are psychologically disconnected from their work.

This isn't a skills problem. It's not an education problem. It's not "some people being lazy." It's disengagement on a scale that drags down national productivity and economic potential.

### 2. Corporate Bloat and the Paradox of Efficiency

Ironically, the push for efficiency in a corporation is the very thing that creates inefficiency.

When engagement declines and productivity weakens, most organizations do not respond by restoring agency or responsibility. They respond by adding systems. New workflows, new tools, new metrics, new procedures, and new layers of management are introduced in the name of efficiency. Employees are assumed to need clearer rules, tighter oversight, and more standardized processes. So control increases. And with it, bureaucracy.

This creates a paradox. The pursuit of efficiency generates administrative overhead that consumes the very productivity it is meant to protect. As complexity grows, more time is spent navigating process than creating value. Initiative gives way to permission-seeking. Judgment is replaced by compliance. Responsibility is displaced upward into committees, dashboards, and escalation chains. The original problem — weakened agency and disengagement — is never addressed. It is managed around.

Over time, disengagement produces bureaucracy, and bureaucracy deepens disengagement. Static roles lead to declining ownership. Declining ownership invites more control. More control reduces discretion. Reduced discretion further weakens motivation. A feedback loop forms in which organizations grow heavier, slower, and more expensive in order to compensate for the energy they have engineered out of their own workforce. What appears as sophistication from the outside is often stagnation on the inside.

### 3. Misallocated Labor Force

Decades of psychological research suggest humans are already wired for development, mastery, and agency. When those drives are supported, motivation emerges naturally. Which means most disengagement is not a failure of effort. It is a failure of alignment.

This is not an argument against stability. Many people prefer predictable work. It is an argument against systems that deny growth to those who seek it.

Modern organizations sort people into roles primarily through credentials, hard skills, and past job titles — legible signals that scale but poorly capture deeper drivers such as persuasion instinct, creative bias, risk tolerance, or strategic orientation. A person naturally wired for sales may begin in finance and never discover where they would thrive. Once inside a system, this initial classification hardens into identity. Internal mobility requires proof of prior experience in the very domains employees have never been allowed to explore. To change direction, they must often "apply like everyone else," competing against candidates already credentialed for the role. Misalignment becomes durable.

The result is a quiet misallocation of human potential. People capable of building are assigned to maintain. People inclined to improve are evaluated on compliance. Over time, they adapt. They stop offering ideas that go nowhere. They stop investing emotionally in work they do not control. What appears as disengagement is often learned irrelevance — the recognition that one can perform, but not meaningfully shape outcomes.

At scale, this represents a massive structural inefficiency. Societies invest heavily in education and talent development, then deploy that capacity into roles optimized for stability rather than growth. Productivity suffers, innovation slows, and vast reservoirs of adaptive human energy remain unused.

It is a form of systemic capability suppression.

Which leads me to ask: what does it look like to support human capability as an organizational goal?

## Where Human Capitalism Goes From Here

This would be a philosophical problem if the world were stable.

It isn't. It's dynamic.

Technology is accelerating. Entire industries are being restructured by AI, automation, and platform shifts. Creative destruction is no longer episodic — it is continuous. The future of work is not static. It is fluid, fast, and uncertain.

Even global institutions acknowledge this. The World Economic Forum repeatedly identifies the most in-demand capabilities of the coming decade as adaptability, resilience, creative thinking, systems reasoning, and learning agility. In other words: dynamic skills.

Those skills can't be articulated on a resume.

We are being told the future belongs to flexible, self-directed, continuously developing people.

And yet our systems still condition people for stability, narrow optimization, and role preservation.

That asymmetry is unsustainable.

You cannot demand adaptability from workers while systematically stripping them of agency.

You cannot expect resilience from people whose development has been outsourced to corporate ladders.

You cannot prepare a workforce for creative destruction while designing careers around static identity and risk avoidance.

This is the cost of drifting from dynamic creation to static preservation. What was once a system of builders became a system of maintainers.

If the future is dynamic — and it clearly is — then agency is no longer optional. It is infrastructure.

Restoring agency is not a lifestyle preference. It is an economic necessity.

But this is not a one-sided problem.

Organizations engineered many of the static conditions we now experience. They centralized decisions, narrowed roles, and optimized for predictability.

Individuals adapted to those conditions. They traded autonomy for security. They allowed early classifications to harden into permanent identities. They deferred exploration because the system rewarded compliance.

Both sides participated in the drift.

Both must participate in the correction.
