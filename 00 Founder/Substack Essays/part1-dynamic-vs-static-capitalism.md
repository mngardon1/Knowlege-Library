# Part 1: The Promise
## Dynamic vs Static Capitalism — The Core Promise and How We Broke It
*Michael Gardon | Feb 10, 2026*

### Series Intro

I was recently interviewed by a journalist on the future of capitalism, and it sparked a deep dive into my thoughts and POV. This is a five-ish part series exploring the central tension:

Capitalism has drifted into something it was never supposed to be—and millions of capable people feel like it's not working for them. They're right. But the answer isn't to abandon capitalism. It's to restore the mechanism that made it thrive.

Both corporations and workers broke this. Both must fix it.

This series explores how.

> *To own the fruits of your labor, you must also bear the responsibility for your decisions in shaping your own outcomes.*

---

If you're sitting down to read this today - you likely feel trapped in your career. You're likely questioning the promise you accepted early on - the promise of opportunity and success and upward mobility. And grappling with what pursuing that promise has cost you.

Is it the system's fault?

Today, millions of capable, hard-working people feel like capitalism isn't working for them.

And they're right to feel that way.

But not for the reasons they think.

My own personal hypothesis is that the root of this feeling is a disconnect in three main drivers of human ingenuity: 1) Risk awareness, 2) proximity to outcome (the "I did that" feeling), and 3) agency (ability to change course and improve personal outcomes).

Part 1 of this series seeks to lay bare how capitalism's core promise has changed, how individuals have come to accept this altered promise, and why it has created the conflict most of us feel today.

## The Dynamic vs. Static Promise

To begin, we need to realize that capitalism is not a system created by a few men in isolation. It is an evolution in the natural order of things. Skill, ability, resource allocation, innovation, comparative advantage, drive to improve our circumstances - are innate human growth and survival qualities that need no formal system in order to flourish.

Capitalism simply says, if we facilitate human ingenuity, human creative problem solving and human drive to excel - good things will happen for the masses.

Capitalism, as a system, only needed to be formalized in order to combat unnatural control over how those qualities were directed.

The enemy was a centralized feudal system that told people what to do, suppressed freedom and broke the human spirit. The feudal goal was to perpetuate what already existed, and extract the maximum from it.

*(If that sounds like what you're living through now, I know, and we'll get there).*

---

## The Dynamic Promise

As codified by Smith, Locke and others, capitalism is a dynamic, self correcting system that promises opportunity (not outcomes) for all. By distributing the ownership of "the means of production" into private hands.

Because you "own the fruits of your labor" in a material sense, you are free to innovate and pursue opportunity as you see fit. If you were right, you reaped the rewards. If you were wrong, or simply unfortunate, you could fall on hard times, but the dynamic system would have you your feet again because the system would keep growing and offering new opportunities.

The entrepreneurs of today innately understand this concept.

As innovation spread, companies were formed to build new plows, create machinery and support the main industries of their times. This created new opportunities for humans to grow and use their talents in new ways.

For over 200 years, this basic way of operating worked. Give the people freedom to direct their "animal spirits" and good things happened. By any metric - standard of living, GDP, wealth, employment, poverty, death rates - "capitalism" has made the entire world a better place.

The first person who decided not to farm his own land, but to build plows to sell to other farmers - he is the reason I can sit in a coffee shop today and write these words.

That dynamic promise was strong for the courageous, but what about everyone else?

## The Dynamic Employment Promise

This "fruits of your labor" argument holds in the material (capital) sense, but I also believe the framers meant it in the psychological sense as well. If you labored on a farm, or produced a widget for someone else, you "own" that capability and you own connection to the outcome.

Not everyone can be or should be an entrepreneur. If you did not, or could not pursue an opportunity in the entrepreneurial sense, an implied contract existed where you could still produce value, find opportunity and pursue your own growth path. In this contract, you trade ownership of your production for wages. The employment relationship was good for both parties. Owners got access to labor to scale the means of production while employees received more predictability and were insulated somewhat from systemic risks of owning a business.

This relationship assumed people understood this fundamental truth: the employment relationship was meant to be temporary and conditional. Both sides enter it with eyes open. The employer could fire a worker if business conditions change, and the worker built capability that travels with him - free to exit and take his talents elsewhere, or open up a competing shop down the street. Both parties maintain responsibility for understanding the risks and tradeoffs they're taking.

The dynamic promise worked because it assumed three things at an individual level (both employee and employer):

- **Symmetric risk** — both sides have risk. The risks are fair and discoverable
- **Value connection** — individuals are connected to the value they create
- **Distributed agency** — individuals are responsible for their own decisions and have power to change

A hired blacksmith banged out a horseshoe, saw it as complete and watched it get put on a horse. They had a direct connection between their work and the good it did in the world.

He saw his contribution, and had a sense of purpose and confidence in his ability.

Yet he understood that his livelihood was in part dependent on how the business did. He accepted this as reality, so he kept tabs on how the business was doing. The blacksmith could see orders coming and going. He had a better pulse on the health of the business, and therefore was naturally aware of risks the business faced, and by extension - the risks he faced as an individual.

He could then choose a new path, recommend a change or create better quality. He had agency.

This relationship wasn't perfect. But it was dynamic.

Capability flowed to where it was needed. Bad decisions got punished quickly. Workers who saw trouble coming could leave. Businesses that wasted talent lost people and adapted or died.

The system stayed alive through constant reallocation.

## Shift To The Static Promise

The dynamic promise worked incredibly well. Innovation flourished, industries were created and destroyed, resources re-allocated. Status quo challenged. New markets opened.

The innate drive to create, and the feeling of accomplishment from seeing your creation in the world won.

Vast amounts of wealth and value were created.

But as businesses grew and consolidated, they expanded into other industries. Their businesses got more complex and interconnected. They hired more employees to manage the complexity.

To fuel the need for people, the dynamic employment promise slowly changed.

Jobs turned into silos. Silos turned into career paths.

Paths offered long term employment and stability - and a sense that companies could take care of their people - as long as the people did what the company wanted.

As careers developed and got more specialized, the climbers received more money and more responsibility, yet became distanced from the actual value produced.

Instead of valuing individual ownership and ingenuity (the dynamic promise), a more static promise emerged for employees:

> *your livelihood is secure. As long as you create value in ways that perpetuate the company, you will be taken care of.*

That promise is a seductive one: less uncertainty, more stability, still growth opportunity.

More and more people gave up their rights to "own the fruits of their labor."

And a conveyor belt was created through the educational system to produce employable individuals.

As society adapted, the promise became more certain.

Go to college, pick a major, learn a narrow skill, get hired, and success is inevitable.

College became the entry fee to the employment path, everything else followed.

In this relentless societal push for certainty, risk asymmetry developed.

Specialization of skill gave people fewer paths to change (the resume trap). Provisions were put in place to make it more difficult for workers to leave. Contracts and non-competes were developed. Consulting or "moonlighting" was explicitly banned. Proprietary know how became a protected asset.

Each iteration of the promise removed uncertainty for the worker, but at the expense of their optionality.

Being an employee is not wrong. The employee promise works. It creates vast amounts of opportunities, and has raised the standard of living for countless individuals.

The asymmetry is wrong. Under the surface, this shift has created the conditions for what many feel today by eroding the core conditions that make capitalism work.

Both employer and employee are at fault. And both need to change.

---

## Static Suffocation

To put it more plainly, the shift from dynamic to static promise in the capitalistic system broke three main conditions that are necessary not only for an equitable system, but that are fundamentally necessary for human flourishing.

### Symmetric Risk

Employers take on risk to deliver goods and services. This can't be understated. Companies need to pursue profit, and have flexibility to lay off employees when market conditions or strategy warrants the change. This is not bad. It's reality. However, it is insane (to me) for high risk decision makers to be insulated from the results of their decisions if they go bad. CEOs should be fired, replaced and have compensation clawed back for underperformance in transparent ways.

As outlined above, companies have made it more difficult for individuals to leave bad situations and take their value elsewhere - transferring more risk to the employee.

At the same time, most employees are walking around "risk blind" - uninformed of strategic or systemic risk to their employer. They've been promised that they'll be taken care of, and thus restructuring and layoffs come as shocks the individuals are rarely prepared for.

All this leads to a feeling of asymmetry: the employee is exposed to more risk than they know, and decision makers escape consequence.

### Value Connection

Through layers of bureaucracy and abstraction the average knowledge worker does not have a fundamental connection between what they produce and the ultimate end value the company produces. We spend 70% of our waking time working, but can't articulate "to what end?" How does our work directly contribute to making the world a better place?

If we can't answer this question concretely, a conflict boils under the surface. We can call this conflict the crisis of meaning.

It shows up as:

- 77% disengaged at work
- 60% emotionally detached
- 23% would take a pay cut for meaningful work

And results in $8 Trillion in estimated productivity loss per year, a mental health crisis and millions of people coming in under their human potential.

### Distributed Agency

The current promise pretends to deliver certainty, but can't. As a result it has confused responsibility in the minds of employees. Responsibility for the individual is never promised by the company, yet it is implied. If the company promises a clear career, isn't it responsible for delivering? If that career doesn't turn out as promised, who's responsibility is it to fix? And if it's the company's responsibility, what am I exactly responsible for? Moreover, if they deliver, but I want a change, don't I owe it to them to stay?

We've mistakenly consolidated our expectations of responsibility in the company as part of the implied promise. Turns out responsibility and agency are two sides of the same coin. So, along with giving up responsibility, we simultaneously give up agency to change.

Each individual is responsible for his or her own choices, at every level, thus each has the agency to change as they see fit.

But right now agency is a foreign term. Even if we believe in it, we feel powerless to execute it inside of the system.

---

## Static Consequences

If an individual bears more risk than she is aware of, can't stand firmly behind the value of their existence, and doesn't have the agency to change her circumstances - the future feels overwhelming. When things get overwhelming, we can't see viable pathways to shape our circumstances, even if they are all around us.

The result is stagnation:

Today, we have a large class of employees who are accomplished and capable, yet own nothing (in terms of output from their time and energy investment).

We have millions of smart, capable and creative individuals who spend 70% of their waking lives working, yet feel consistently disconnected, apathetic and stuck.

We see the excitement of innovation, yet our livelihoods are threatened by it.

We have objectively more opportunity, yet subjectively fewer pathways.

We have more information at our fingertips yet less ability to translate that information into action.

We're busier than ever, yet devoid of outcome.

We live in the most advanced time in history yet worse physical and mental health.

We still have our innate drive to create, be valued and control our own destiny, yet less will to let go of what holds us back.

**We are dynamic yearners, stuck in a static model - and it's killing us.**

---

## The Feudal Result

What's apparent to me today is that free, distributed and competitive capitalism has morphed back into something that more closely resembles the feudal tyranny Smith and Locke were trying to escape.

A few, mega pockets of power changing the rules and setting the agenda, and a majority employee underclass who blindly accepts an asymmetric promise in the name of security.

We've allowed our value to be named by someone else, and directed when and where to be deployed. A society religiously following the dangled carrot, yet wondering why work feels hollow.

Yes, we have far more freedom and ownership than existed in the 1700's - it's not even close.

Yes, even larger amounts of value and extraordinary wealth have been created during this shift. Both are great for society at large.

I'm not arguing that this shift is all bad. What is just now appearing harmful is the trade we've made. Lost sovereignty and agency for a certain path that has turned out to be anything but certain.

And that tension is bubbling up under the surface.
