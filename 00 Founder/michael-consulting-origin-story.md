# Michael Gardon — Consulting Origin Story & Philosophy
*Knowledge Asset | Founder Experience | Last updated March 2026*

---

## The Raw History

**2012 — First consulting company, post-startup failure**
- Age ~30–32. Just came off a startup that didn't work.
- Formed an LLC, picked a name, started cold.
- Moved Chicago → Madison, Wisconsin.
- Strategy: land small business / startup clients, learn their problems, then join one of them as an employee.
- This was deliberately a portfolio play — consulting as a vehicle to find the next thing, not as the destination.

**Offer at the time:** Finance consulting, operations consulting, content marketing consulting.

**Result:** Three clients within a month. Revenue immediately exceeded prior income. Got hired as employee #5 at a startup. Helped build it into a large company.

---

**~2016 — Back to consulting (involuntarily)**
- Got fired from the startup.
- Returned to the original LLC — hadn't touched it in ~4 years.
- Started networking and offering consulting again.
- That pipeline of activity led to a full-time role in corporate innovation at a major insurance company.

---

**~2019–2021 — Used consulting to exit corporate**
- Three years into corporate innovation role.
- Used the existing LLC shell as the vehicle.
- Built a new offer: consulting with large internet content marketing companies in the affiliate marketing space, specifically finance.
- Landed one high-value, long-term client who effectively bridged him out of corporate.
- Simultaneously building owned affiliate/content assets in parallel.
- That combination — consulting revenue + asset income — is how he got out.

---

## The Core Insight (In His Words)

> "If you have your own consulting company, you're able to move and shift and adapt very fast to changing circumstances."

> "My point of view is that basically everyone who works in corporate should have a consulting company to be able to get revenue in times of change."

> "Once you start to do that in the external world, you're able to really understand how to sell. And that's a key skill that is lacking in most people. And that's the reason why they are so hesitant to get out on their own."

## What This Means Strategically

### The consulting company as optionality infrastructure
Michael didn't start consulting because he wanted to be a consultant. He started it as a hedge, a bridge, and a vehicle — and it became the mechanism that gave him agency across three major career transitions.

This is the real argument for The Upstream Consultant: not "consulting is your dream" but "consulting is the most flexible, portable, revenue-generating structure available to a knowledge worker."

### Selling is the missing skill
The reason most corporate professionals resist going independent isn't capability. It's that they've never had to sell. Corporate abstracts that away. Consulting forces it — and once you learn it, the fear dissolves.

### The portfolio play mindset
Michael's first consulting company wasn't designed to be a lifestyle business. It was designed to create options. That framing — consulting as a vehicle for agency, not just income — is a core differentiator in how Upstream is positioned vs. generic "start a consulting business" content.

## The Perfect Client — money.com

- Client had acquired money.com (the old Money Magazine brand) and was building out the digital asset
- Michael's job: build content and marketing infrastructure for the digital expansion
- Key dynamic: **he could move faster than they could internally** — the consulting structure was the unlock, not just the expertise
- This is the clearest proof that positioning around speed + external flexibility beats being a full-time hire

## What He Got Wrong (The Regret That Became Upstream)

**Being a generalist.**

> "If I could do anything differently, it would have been to not be a generalist. I should have been much more specific in my domain and offering. Created a position in the market and a brand."

- Avoided the trap of running a marketing shop (too many clients, too many employees) — that was intentional and right
- But didn't build career capital or a market position — that was the cost of generalism
- Today: "It would be much easier if I had a real brand around my business and real marketing assets on socials"

This is the origin of the Positioning Audit. Michael learned the hard way what happens when you skip it.

**What he knows now — the specific offer as beachhead:**

A specific, repeatable offer doesn't box you in. It does the opposite:
- Shortcuts conversations with prospects — they immediately understand what problem you solve
- Gets you to selling faster instead of explaining longer
- Once the relationship is established, you can expand scope and become stickier
- Generalism at the start means you spend the whole conversation explaining instead of selling

> "If you're general at first, you waste a ton of time explaining instead of selling."

The beachhead offer is not a ceiling. It's an entry point. The generalist tries to show everything at once and closes nothing. The specialist gets in the door, then expands.

## On "Consulting" vs. "Freelancing" — The Terminology Problem

Michael's POV (use this directly in content):

> "I think consulting is more authoritative. People hear freelancing and they think you do one specific job or play one specific role."

> "I honestly would like to come up with a better term than consulting — but for now that one works best."

**The real definition he's working toward:**
A consulting company gives you the opportunity to earn revenue:
- In between full-time jobs
- On top of full-time jobs
- In place of full-time jobs

> "Instead of sitting around looking for new work and preparing for interviews, you're able to go out with something real and actually sell it in the marketplace."

This is the frame. Consulting company = active revenue vehicle. Job search = passive waiting.
